Agentic Commerce

The condition in which AI agents intermediate discovery, comparison, trust assessment, and purchase routing on behalf of buyers — making machine-readable merchant identity and governed commerce infrastructure prerequisites for commercial visibility.

What Is Agentic Commerce?

Commerce has always been mediated. Merchants sell through channels; channels connect merchants to buyers; buyers choose among the options a channel presents. The identity of the mediator has changed across centuries — from bazaar merchants, to catalogues, to search engines, to social feeds. Agentic commerce names the next transition: AI systems that act as autonomous or semi-autonomous intermediaries between buyer intent and merchant selection.

In agentic commerce, the buyer does not navigate a marketplace or type a query into a search engine. The buyer delegates. An AI agent receives the buyer's intent — "find me a reliable supplier for this product under these conditions" — and the agent searches, evaluates, compares, and in some architectures, transacts. The buyer receives a result; the merchant either appears in that result or does not.

This is not a hypothetical future. AI shopping assistants, product research agents, and purchase-routing systems are already deployed across consumer and B2B commerce. The trajectory is toward deeper agent involvement in the purchase path, not less. What matters now — before the transition is complete — is how merchants position themselves to be found, evaluated, and trusted by systems that cannot be sold to with advertising and cannot be convinced with a good story. Agents read data. Merchants must become readable.

How AI Agents Route Commerce

An AI agent evaluating a merchant operates differently from a human buyer. It does not respond to brand aesthetics, sales language, or social proof in the form of testimonials. It reads structured signals. The process typically moves through four layers:

  • Discovery — Can the agent find the merchant? Is the merchant's catalog and identity present in machine-readable form outside any single platform's proprietary database? Does the merchant have a canonical URL that persists regardless of which data source the agent queries?
  • Evaluation — Can the agent read the merchant's offerings, prices, terms, and conditions in structured form? Is the product data complete and machine-parseable (JSON-LD, structured schema, stable data formats)? Are the merchant's policies — returns, disputes, shipping — stated clearly enough that an agent can represent them to a buyer?
  • Trust Assessment — Does the merchant have portable, machine-readable trust signals? Are reviews structured as data, not only as platform-native ratings? Is there verifiable transaction history outside any single platform? Are identity records stable and consistent across sources?
  • Routing — If the agent decides to route the buyer to the merchant, can it do so reliably? Is there a stable, canonical purchase path that does not require the agent to navigate a proprietary marketplace interface?

A merchant who passes all four layers for an AI agent is what the Outmerchant Lexicon defines as an Agent-Selectable Merchant. A merchant who fails at any layer may become less visible to agents that cannot find them through their own proprietary data access — which typically means visibility remains concentrated inside platforms the merchant already sells through.

The Merchant Sovereignty Problem

Agentic commerce does not solve the platform dependency problem. It intensifies it.

A merchant who sells primarily through a large marketplace benefits, in principle, from being in that marketplace's catalog — because agents that query that marketplace will find them. But this is the same dependency in new form. The merchant is not agent-selectable; they are platform-discoverable-by-agents. Their visibility is contingent on the platform's relationship with the agent ecosystem, on the platform's data access policies, and on whether the agent has been trained or configured to query that platform at all.

The sovereign condition is different: the merchant's identity, catalog, and trust signals exist in machine-readable form that agents are more likely to discover and evaluate without passing through a proprietary platform intermediary. This requires owning a canonical presence — a stable domain with structured data, governed information architecture, and portable trust signals — that exists and is maintained independently of any marketplace relationship.

This is not a technical problem with a technical solution. It is a merchant sovereignty problem: who controls the infrastructure that makes the merchant discoverable, evaluable, and trustable. In agentic commerce, that infrastructure is machine-readable governed data. Merchants who own it are sovereign in the agent layer. Merchants who do not are platform-dependent in the agent layer, even if they sell across multiple human-facing channels.

What Makes a Merchant Agent-Selectable

Agent-selectability is a measurable condition. It requires four infrastructure elements, each of which can be built independently and measured as a dimension of merchant sovereignty:

Machine-Readable Catalog

Products, prices, availability, and terms expressed in structured data formats (JSON-LD, schema.org markup) that any agent can parse without requiring platform-specific access or human intermediation. Not just a web page; a data layer.

Canonical Identity

A stable, consistent commercial identity that exists at a canonical URL outside any single platform — a permanent address where the merchant's identity, terms, and catalog can be reliably found and cited by any evaluating system.

Portable Trust Signals

Reviews, ratings, dispute resolution records, and transaction history that exist in structured, portable form — accessible to evaluating agents without querying a proprietary platform's database. Trust that travels with the merchant, not trust that belongs to a marketplace.

Governed Commerce Terms

Return policies, shipping commitments, dispute procedures, and operational rules stated clearly and stably — so that an AI agent can accurately represent them to a buyer and rely on them not changing unpredictably between the moment of evaluation and the moment of transaction.

These four elements together constitute what the Outmerchant Lexicon calls Machine-Readable Trust: the condition in which a merchant's credibility infrastructure is structured for AI evaluation, not only for human perception.

The Outmerchant Dimension: AI Commerce Readiness

07 / AGT — 12.5% of the Merchant Sovereignty Score

AI Commerce Readiness

Dimension 07 of the Merchant Sovereignty Score measures the degree to which a merchant's commercial infrastructure is structured for agent-mediated discovery and trust: whether the catalog is machine-readable, whether commercial identity is canonical and platform-independent, whether trust signals are portable and structured, and whether governance terms are stable enough for AI systems to rely on.

The three governed questions in this dimension assess:

  • Whether your commercial identity exists in machine-readable, structured form outside any single platform's database.
  • Whether your product catalog, pricing, and terms are accessible to AI systems without platform-specific access.
  • Whether your trust signals — reviews, transaction history, dispute records — are portable or locked inside proprietary systems.

A low score on this dimension does not mean a merchant is failing at commerce. It means a merchant is invisible to the portion of commerce that is now agent-mediated — and that portion is growing, not shrinking. The merchants who address this dimension now are building agent-era infrastructure before the transition makes it urgent.

Agentic Commerce and the Route to the Buyer

The central principle of the Outmerchant standard is that Route-to-Buyer Control — the condition in which the merchant owns, rather than rents, the infrastructure between themselves and their buyers — is the foundation of commercial sovereignty.

Agentic commerce adds a new layer to this principle. The route to the buyer is not only a human-navigable path anymore. It is also a machine-traversable path: a data infrastructure that AI agents can query, evaluate, and route through. A merchant who owns their human route but not their machine route has partial sovereignty — and the machine route is the one with the higher growth trajectory.

The Outmerchant framework names the condition of fully sovereign route-to-buyer control in the agentic era as Agentic Commerce Readiness: the degree to which a merchant has structured their commercial infrastructure for agent-mediated discovery, evaluation, trust, and transaction — not as a supplementary channel, but as a primary layer of the route to the buyer that no single platform controls.

Audit your commercial identity outside any single platform. Frequently Asked Questions

What is agentic commerce?

Agentic commerce is the condition in which AI agents intermediate discovery, comparison, trust assessment, and purchase routing on behalf of buyers. Instead of a buyer manually searching and choosing, an AI agent does the searching, evaluating, and in some cases transacting — on the buyer's behalf. For merchants, it means the route to the buyer now passes through AI systems that must be able to read, evaluate, and trust the merchant independently.

How do AI shopping agents choose which merchants to buy from?

AI agents evaluate merchants across catalog completeness and machine-readability, identity stability (consistent canonical presence outside any single platform), portable trust signals (structured reviews, dispute records, policies), and governance stability (terms that are clear and don't change unpredictably). Merchants who exist only inside proprietary platform databases are accessible only to agents querying those platforms. Merchants with a canonical, structured, platform-independent presence are accessible to any agent.

What is an agent-selectable merchant?

An agent-selectable merchant is a merchant whose commercial identity, catalog, reputation, and governance are structured in ways that allow AI agents to discover, evaluate, and trust them without requiring human intermediation or platform-specific access. It is the end-state condition that Dimension 07 (AGT) of the Merchant Sovereignty Score measures toward.

How do I make my store visible to AI shopping agents?

Four moves: (1) Add JSON-LD structured data to your product and organization pages. (2) Establish a canonical domain presence with a stable URL structure that persists outside any marketplace. (3) Structure trust signals (reviews, policies, dispute records) as machine-readable data. (4) Publish clear, stable commerce terms that agents can rely on. The Merchant Sovereignty Score measures your current AI Commerce Readiness as Dimension 07 (AGT).

What is machine-readable trust in commerce?

Machine-readable trust is the condition in which a merchant's credibility signals are structured as machine-parseable data rather than only as human-readable prose or proprietary platform records. An AI agent evaluating a merchant reads structured data. A merchant whose trust signals exist only inside a single platform's database has machine-readable trust only on that platform — which is no trust at all from any agent that queries a different source.

How does agentic commerce affect merchant sovereignty?

Agentic commerce creates a new control layer in the route to the buyer. Previously, sovereignty required owning channels that humans could find and use. Now it also requires owning machine-legibility — the ability to be found, evaluated, and trusted by AI systems operating on behalf of buyers. A merchant fully sovereign in human-readable channels but invisible to AI agents has a critical gap in route-to-buyer control. The Outmerchant framework measures this gap through Dimension 07 (AI Commerce Readiness).
