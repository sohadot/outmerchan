Captured: Sovereignty Tier 0–25

0 — 25

What This Level Means

Captured is the lowest tier of the Merchant Sovereignty Score. It names the condition in which the infrastructure owns the merchant: the platform holds the margin, the reputation, the customer relationship, and the route to the buyer. The merchant operates — but only at the infrastructure's pleasure.

The standard's lexicon calls the mechanism platform capture: the end-state of unmanaged platform dependency. A merchant scores in this tier when the combination of dependency, locked reputation, absent customer ownership, and constrained governance independence leaves them structurally unable to operate without the platform's active cooperation.

Capture Is a Control Condition, Not a Revenue Condition

The most important and counterintuitive fact about the Captured tier: captured merchants are often profitable. Some are highly profitable. Revenue is not the indicator of capture. Control is.

A merchant whose business produces strong revenue through a single marketplace is in the Captured tier if that marketplace controls the margin (sets the fees), controls the visibility (sets the ranking), controls the customer relationship (owns the buyer data), and controls the right to operate (can suspend the account). Profitability in this condition is contingent: it continues only as long as the platform's commercial interests align with the merchant's.

When those interests diverge — through a fee increase, an algorithm change, a new private label product competing directly with the merchant's, a policy update, or a suspension — the profitable condition ends faster than most merchants can respond. The profitable-but-captured merchant is often the most vulnerable precisely because high revenue suppresses the urgency to build alternatives before they are needed.

The Three Vectors of Capture

Capture operates through three distinct mechanisms, each of which can trap a merchant independently — and all of which are typically present in the Captured tier:

Revenue Concentration

More than 80% of revenue through one platform. No meaningful diversification. A platform decision that affects revenue in that channel affects the entire business.

Reputation Hostage

Years of reviews, ratings, and transaction history trapped inside one proprietary database. Not portable. Leave the platform — or be removed — and the credibility record disappears.

Route Capture

The buyer relationship is owned by the platform. The merchant does not know who bought from them, cannot contact their buyers directly, and cannot serve them outside the platform's ecosystem.

Addressing one vector without the others does not exit the Captured tier. A merchant who diversifies revenue but whose reputation is still hostage and whose customer relationship is still platform-mediated remains vulnerable to the two vectors they have not addressed. The Merchant Sovereignty Score measures all three vectors across its eight dimensions.

Who Falls Into This Tier

Typically: marketplace-only sellers, social-commerce sellers whose entire funnel lives inside one app, and merchants whose storefront, payments, fulfillment, and customer contact all run through a single provider stack. Often successful in volume — capture is a control condition, not a revenue condition. Many captured merchants are profitable right up until the decision that ends them.

The common profile: a business that started on a marketplace because the marketplace gave easy access to buyers, scaled successfully within that marketplace's ecosystem, and optimized for marketplace performance rather than sovereignty. The optimization was rational in the short term. The structural risk accumulated silently.

The Common Control Pattern

In the Captured tier, the control pattern follows a recognizable structure: one dominant channel carries the revenue; the buyer database belongs to the platform; reviews and transaction history exist only inside one proprietary system; all payment processing flows through a single gateway the merchant did not choose; the rules of commerce are set by the platform's terms of service and change without negotiation.

The merchant in this condition makes operational decisions inside a set of constraints they do not control. Pricing decisions are bounded by platform fee structures. Marketing decisions are bounded by platform advertising systems. Product decisions are influenced by platform category rules. Communication with buyers is mediated by platform messaging systems.

Trigger Events That End Captured Merchants

The Captured tier does not typically end gradually. It ends through events — decisions made above the merchant's head that convert a viable business into a crisis faster than alternatives can be built:

  • Account suspension — often without warning, often without meaningful appeal. Policy violations, IP complaints, or algorithmic flags can suspend an account indefinitely. A captured merchant with no alternative route stops selling the same day.
  • Algorithm change — organic visibility collapses. What ranked on page one is now invisible. Platform advertising costs rise to compensate, compressing margin toward zero.
  • Fee increase — retroactive margin compression. Commission rates, fulfillment costs, and advertising minimums increase. The merchant cannot renegotiate; they can comply, absorb the loss, or exit.
  • Platform market exit — a platform leaves a country, closes a category, or shuts down entirely. The merchant's commerce in that market ends on the platform's timeline.
  • New competition from the platform itself — the platform introduces a private label product competing directly with the merchant's best seller, using its own visibility controls to preference its product over the merchant's.
  • Terms of service change — rules change without negotiation. What was permitted yesterday is restricted today. The merchant adapts or exits.
The Captured Merchant's Paradox

The highest-revenue period is often the highest-risk period. A merchant optimizing for a platform that controls them is building the infrastructure for an exit they cannot execute. Revenue signals safety. Control signals vulnerability. The Merchant Sovereignty Score measures control, not revenue.

The Path Toward OutMerchant

Movement out of Captured starts with the two dimensions that loosen the platform's grip fastest: Platform Dependency and Customer Ownership. Open one channel no third party can switch off, and start holding consented, direct buyer contact. Re-measure. The path runs through Dependent and Emerging — and its terminal state is the rank that carries the standard's name: OutMerchant (76–100).

The sequence is not optional. A merchant who diversifies revenue without building owned buyer contact remains captured through route capture. A merchant who builds an email list but keeps 90% of revenue on one platform remains captured through revenue concentration. The standard's eight-dimension architecture exists precisely because capture operates across multiple vectors simultaneously, and all must be addressed to genuinely exit the tier.

Can a profitable merchant be captured?

Yes — and this is the defining danger of the Captured tier. Capture is a control condition, not a revenue condition. Many captured merchants are profitable until a trigger event (account suspension, algorithm change, fee increase) converts their dependency into a crisis they cannot absorb because they have no alternative routes. What are the signs I am in the Captured tier?

Primary signals: more than 80% of revenue through one platform; no owned channel no third party can switch off; buyer contact mediated entirely by the platform; reputation locked in one proprietary database; no plan for what to do if your largest platform suspended your account today. The Merchant Sovereignty Score measures this precisely across all eight dimensions.

What trigger events end a captured merchant's business?

Account suspension (often without appeal), algorithm changes that collapse visibility, retroactive fee increases, platform market exits, new private-label competition from the platform itself, and terms of service changes that prohibit previous practices. In each case the captured merchant has no resilient alternative route to absorb the event. The event that is an inconvenience for a sovereign merchant is an extinction event for a captured one.

What is the difference between Captured and Dependent?

Captured (0–25) means no meaningful alternative route exists and all three vectors of capture apply. Dependent (26–50) means the merchant has begun building alternatives — a secondary channel, some direct buyer contact — but not enough to provide genuine resilience. A platform suspension would still be a severe crisis for a dependent merchant. The score measures exactly where the gap is largest across all eight dimensions.
