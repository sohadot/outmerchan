Dependent: Sovereignty Tier 26–50

26 — 50

What This Level Means

Dependent is the second tier of the Merchant Sovereignty Score. The merchant is partially free: they operate real channels of their own, but on borrowed infrastructure — platform-dominated revenue, single-rail settlement, and trust that still lives mostly in other people's databases. The cage door is open, but the merchant still sleeps inside.

The Borrowed Infrastructure Condition

Borrowed infrastructure is the defining condition of the Dependent tier. It does not mean the merchant is building nothing of their own — it means the foundation they are building on is not theirs to keep. The customer database their marketplace maintains, the five-star history their platform holds, the payment network their processor controls: all of these are assets the merchant uses but does not own.

The danger is not that borrowed infrastructure is unavailable. It is always available — until the provider decides it is not. A terms change, a rate increase, an account suspension, an acquisition: any of these can reprice or revoke the infrastructure a Dependent merchant has spent years relying on. The merchant's business is real. Its foundation is rented.

Who Falls Into This Tier

Typically: merchants with their own storefront whose revenue is still dominated by one marketplace; DTC brands that own their site but depend on one payment provider and one ad platform for nearly all discovery; sellers who hold some customer data but could not survive their primary channel's hostility.

Dependent vs. Captured: The Critical Difference

Captured (0–25)

Commerce is entirely platform-mediated. No direct customer contact. No alternative settlement rail. No independent trust signal. Cannot leave without starting from zero. The cage door is shut.

Dependent (26–50)

Real infrastructure exists but is secondary. Has some direct customer relationships. Has a second payment option. Has a storefront — but the marketplace still carries most revenue. The cage door is open; the merchant just hasn't structurally left.

The distinction matters because the risk profile is different. A Captured merchant faces existential threat from a single platform decision. A Dependent merchant faces serious damage — losing the primary channel is devastating, not terminal, if they have genuinely begun building their own infrastructure. The path is real; the exit is possible; but neither is yet structural.

The Five Signs of the Dependent Tier

  • You have an owned storefront — but it generates less than 30% of total revenue
  • Most of your buyers are only reachable through a platform — you do not hold their direct contact data
  • One payment provider processes more than 80% of your settlements
  • Your verified trading history and reputation live primarily in platform-controlled databases
  • A primary marketplace suspension would cause serious revenue loss with no tested recovery path within 90 days

The Common Control Pattern

Owned channel exists but is secondary. One settlement rail carries everything. A meaningful minority of customers are directly reachable; the majority are not. Reputation partially archived, not independently verifiable. Exit paths known but undocumented.

The Priority Path Out of Dependent

The exit from Dependent is not about doing everything simultaneously — it is about the three moves that produce the most structural improvement per unit of effort:

  • Make the owned channel carry real revenue: The target is not 50% — it is any percentage high enough that a marketplace suspension does not kill the business. For most merchants, 35–40% owned revenue changes the risk calculus entirely.
  • Capture direct customer contact systematically: Every new sale is an opportunity to capture a consented, portable contact. Build the owned list from this quarter forward, not retroactively.
  • Add and genuinely test a second payment rail: The second rail must process real volume — even 10% — to be genuinely tested. A backup that has never processed a real sale is not a backup.

Re-measure after implementing these three changes. Most Dependent merchants who make them deliberately move into the Emerging tier within 12 months.

First Move

This week: calculate what percentage of this month's revenue came from your owned channel. If it is under 20%, that is the number to change first — everything else depends on having a real owned revenue base to stand on.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a dependent merchant?

A dependent merchant is in the second tier of the Merchant Sovereignty Score (26–50): they operate real channels of their own but rely on platform-dominated revenue, single-rail settlement, and customer data that still lives primarily in other platforms' databases. The cage door is open, but the merchant still sleeps inside. They are partially free — but not structurally free.

What is the score range for the Dependent tier?

The Dependent tier covers Merchant Sovereignty Scores of 26–50. It is the second of four tiers: Captured (0–25), Dependent (26–50), Emerging (51–75), and Outmerchant (76–100). These boundaries are fixed under public score governance and cannot be changed without a recorded decision.

What is the difference between the Dependent and Captured tiers?

Captured merchants (0–25) have no independent infrastructure — all commerce is fully platform-mediated. Dependent merchants (26–50) have built some real infrastructure (an owned store, some direct customers, perhaps a second payment option) but the primary revenue, customer data, and trust still live in platform-controlled systems. Captured merchants cannot leave without starting from zero. Dependent merchants could leave — but it would cost them most of what they have built.

What are the signs that you are in the Dependent tier?

Five characteristic signs: (1) You have an owned storefront but it generates under 30% of revenue. (2) Most customers are only reachable through a platform. (3) One payment provider handles more than 80% of settlements. (4) Your reputation lives primarily in platform-controlled databases. (5) A primary marketplace suspension would cause serious revenue loss with no tested recovery path within 90 days.

How do you move from the Dependent tier to Emerging?

Three priority moves: (1) Make the owned channel carry 35–40% of revenue — enough that a marketplace suspension does not kill the business. (2) Capture direct, consented customer contact data with every new sale. (3) Add a second payment rail that actively processes real volume. Most Dependent merchants who make these three changes deliberately move into the Emerging tier within 12 months on re-measurement.

What does borrowed infrastructure mean for merchants?

Borrowed infrastructure is the commercial foundation a Dependent merchant uses but does not own or control: the marketplace's customer database, the payment provider's trust network, the platform's discovery algorithm. It is always available — until the provider decides it is not. A terms change, a rate increase, or an acquisition can reprice or revoke infrastructure a merchant has built their business on. 'Borrowed' does not mean free; it means controlled by someone else.

The terminal state of this path carries the standard's name: Outmerchant. The next tier: Emerging (51–75).
