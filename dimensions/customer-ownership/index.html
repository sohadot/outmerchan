Customer Ownership

What This Dimension Means

Customer Ownership measures who holds the relationship with your buyer: you, or the marketplace standing between you. It asks whether you can reach your customers directly, whether they could follow you if you left, and who keeps the master record of the data your own sales generate.

Customer Access vs. Customer Ownership

Customer access is the ability to reach buyers through a platform's interface — sending order updates, responding to inquiries, viewing transaction history in the platform's dashboard. Every marketplace seller has customer access.

Customer ownership is the ability to reach buyers directly, independently of any platform, using contact data the merchant holds and controls. A seller with 10,000 Amazon buyers has customer access. A merchant with 10,000 email subscribers has customer ownership. The distinction is whether the contact requires a platform's permission — and what happens to the relationship when that permission is revoked.

Platform customer data policies make this concrete. Amazon's Seller Agreement prohibits using buyer contact information to solicit sales outside the marketplace. The buyer relationship belongs to Amazon. The seller sees the transaction; the platform owns the relationship. This is not a loophole — it is the design.

Why It Matters to Merchant Sovereignty

The route to the buyer is the most valuable asset a merchant does not own. A customer list inside a platform's database is not your customer list — it is the platform's asset, monetized back to you as advertising. Sovereignty over every other dimension matters little if, at the end of it, the buyer belongs to someone else's algorithm.

The consequences of low customer ownership are asymmetric: everything appears fine until it suddenly is not. An account suspension, a platform exit, or even a policy change around communication can sever a merchant from years of customer relationships with no warning and no recovery path — because the contacts were never theirs to keep.

The Three Layers of Customer Ownership

Layer 1: Contact

Does the merchant hold direct contact data — email, phone, or other reachable identifier — that they collected themselves, with consent, and store in a system they control?

Layer 2: Consent

Is the contact data consented and portable? Consent collected via platform terms may not survive a platform change. First-party consent, collected through the merchant's own channel, belongs to the merchant regardless of what any platform does.

Layer 3: History

Does the merchant hold the master record of their own transaction history — not a dashboard view, but the data itself? Transaction history is leverage: in credit, in insurance, in negotiations, and with AI agents evaluating the merchant's reliability.

How the Score Measures It

The Merchant Sovereignty Score assesses this dimension through 3 governed questions, weighted at 12.5% of the total score. The questions are fixed under public score governance — they change only through a recorded decision, never silently:

  • Can you contact your buyers directly, using contact data you store yourself?
  • If you left your main platform tomorrow, what share of your customers could follow you?
  • Who owns the customer and transaction data your sales generate?

Each answer is scored from captured behavior (0 points) to sovereign behavior (maximum points). The dimension score is the share of sovereign points earned, from 0 to 100.

Low Control vs. High Control

What low control looks like

All buyer contact is mediated by the platform. Leaving means starting from zero customers. The platform sees your full transaction history; you see what its dashboard chooses to show. Contact data belongs to the platform's terms, not yours.

What high control looks like

You can contact nearly all of your buyers directly with consented data you store yourself. Most customers could follow you to a new venue. You hold the master record of your own commerce — transaction history, contact data, and consent — independent of any platform's database.

Customer Ownership in the Agentic Commerce Era

As agentic commerce develops — AI agents researching, evaluating, and routing purchases on behalf of buyers — customer ownership gains a new dimension. A merchant with high customer ownership is more legible to AI agents: first-party data creates verifiable purchase history, independently checkable without platform mediation. A merchant whose entire customer history lives in a platform's database is effectively invisible to agents operating outside that platform's ecosystem.

Customer ownership, in the AI era, is also agent-contact capacity: the merchant's ability to be found, verified, and selected by an AI agent acting for a buyer they already know, without requiring the platform's permission to surface the relationship.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is customer ownership in e-commerce?

Customer ownership in e-commerce is a merchant's ability to contact and maintain relationships with their buyers directly, without requiring a platform's permission or mediation. It is measured by whether the merchant holds consented direct contact data, what share of customers could follow the merchant to a new venue, and who holds the master record of the transaction data the merchant's sales generate.

Do sellers own their customers on Amazon or other marketplace platforms?

No. Amazon's Seller Agreement prohibits using buyer contact information to solicit sales outside the marketplace, and restricts what sellers can do with buyer data. The buyer relationship belongs to the platform. The seller sees the transaction; the platform owns the relationship. This is the defining characteristic of low customer ownership: the buyer came through the platform, the platform mediates all contact, and the platform's rules govern what the seller can do with the relationship.

What is first-party customer data?

First-party customer data is contact and behavioral data collected directly from buyers by the merchant, with consent, through the merchant's own channels — owned storefront, email newsletter, loyalty program, or direct account registration. It contrasts with platform-mediated data, which the merchant can view but does not own. First-party data is portable: if the merchant leaves the platform, the data comes with them. Platform data is not.

What happens to customer data if a marketplace suspends my account?

When a marketplace suspends a seller's account, the buyer contact data and relationship history inside that platform is typically inaccessible or unusable. The seller loses the ability to reach those buyers. For a merchant whose customer relationship is fully platform-mediated, an account suspension effectively means starting over with a customer list of zero. This is the core risk the Customer Ownership dimension measures.

What is the difference between customer access and customer ownership?

Customer access is the ability to reach buyers through a platform's interface. Customer ownership is the ability to reach buyers directly, using contact data the merchant holds and controls. A seller with 10,000 Amazon buyers has customer access. A merchant with 10,000 email subscribers has customer ownership. The distinction is whether reaching those buyers requires a platform's permission — and what happens when that permission is revoked.

How do AI agents interact with customer ownership?

In agentic commerce, AI buying agents research and route purchases on behalf of buyers. A merchant with high customer ownership — first-party data, direct contact, independently verifiable identity — is more legible to AI agents acting for repeat buyers. A merchant whose customer history lives entirely in a platform's database is invisible to agents operating outside that platform's ecosystem. Customer ownership, in the AI era, is also agent-contact capacity.
