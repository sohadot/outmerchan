Reputation Portability

What This Dimension Means

Reputation Portability measures whether your reviews, ratings, and trading history are an asset you can carry — or a hostage held in a platform's proprietary database. It asks whether verifiable proof of your reliability exists anywhere a platform does not control.

Why Platform Reviews Are Not Your Asset

A seller with 5,000 five-star Amazon reviews has not built a reputation. They have built Amazon's reputation. The reviews are displayed on Amazon's platform, stored in Amazon's database, governed by Amazon's review policy, and subject to Amazon's decision about whether to show, hide, or remove them. The trust capital exists — but it belongs to the platform's system, not to the merchant who earned it.

This is not an edge case. Every marketplace review, every platform rating, every in-system trust badge is owned by the platform that issued it. The seller can reference it while they are on the platform. The day the account closes, the trust capital stays behind.

Why It Matters to Merchant Sovereignty

Reputation is the merchant's accumulated trust capital, often built over years of flawless trade. When that capital is locked in one platform's system, it silently converts into the platform's strongest retention weapon: the merchant cannot leave without abandoning the very proof that they deserve to be trusted. Portable reputation reverses that lock.

The Hostage Lock: How Reputation Capture Works

The mechanism is structural, not malicious. Platforms build trust systems to reduce friction for their buyers — not to trap their sellers. But the effect is the same: years of trading history that cannot leave. The longer the merchant has been on a platform, and the better their record, the more their reputation holds them in place. The five-star seller with 10,000 reviews is the merchant most captured by their own success.

The Reputation Hostage Mechanism

The better your platform reputation, the more it costs you to leave. A merchant with 10,000 five-star reviews is more captured than one with 100 — because more of their credibility is held in the platform's system. Reputation lock is not proportional to dependence; it is proportional to excellence.

How the Score Measures It

The Merchant Sovereignty Score assesses this dimension through 2 governed questions, weighted at 12.5% of the total score. The questions are fixed under public score governance — they change only through a recorded decision, never silently:

  • Are your reviews and ratings portable to another venue?
  • Does verifiable proof of your trading history exist outside any single platform's database?

Each answer is scored from captured behavior (0 points) to sovereign behavior (maximum points). The dimension score is the share of sovereign points earned, from 0 to 100.

Low Control vs. High Control

What low control looks like

Years of five-star history that evaporates the day the account closes. No independent record of fulfilled orders. Trust exists only where the platform displays it, in the format the platform allows.

What high control looks like

Trading history archived and provable outside any single venue. Reviews and trust signals verifiable through independent services. A buyer — or an AI agent — can confirm your record without asking the platform's permission.

Three Forms of Independent Reputation

Independent Review Platforms

Reviews on Trustpilot, Google Business, or industry-specific platforms that exist outside any marketplace ecosystem. These survive marketplace account closures and can be referenced independently.

Archived Trading History

Regularly exported and archived transaction records that provide independently verifiable proof of fulfilled orders — volume, completion rate, dispute rate. Not a dashboard screenshot: actual exportable data.

Structured Trust Data

schema.org Organization, AggregateRating, and review markup published on the merchant's own domain. Independently crawlable by search engines and AI agents without platform mediation.

Reputation Portability and AI Agents

As agentic commerce develops, AI buying agents evaluate merchants using machine-readable data: structured markup, independently crawlable review data, and verifiable identity signals. A merchant whose entire reputation lives inside a platform's proprietary system provides no independently parseable trust data for an AI agent evaluating options outside that platform's context.

A merchant with structured data on their own domain, independent reviews, and archived trading history is verifiable by an AI agent without platform mediation. In the agentic commerce era, portable reputation is also agent-readable reputation — the prerequisite for being evaluated and selected by AI agents acting on behalf of buyers who are not starting their search on a platform.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is portable reputation for merchants?

Portable reputation is a merchant's verifiable trading history and trust record that exists independently of any single platform's database — reviews, ratings, and fulfilled order history that can be verified by a buyer or AI agent without asking a platform's permission. The opposite is hostage reputation: trust capital locked in a proprietary system that cannot be transferred, exported, or independently verified.

What happens to my reviews if Amazon suspends my account?

When Amazon suspends a seller's account, the seller loses access to their storefront and the ability to use their reviews to establish credibility elsewhere. The reviews remain on Amazon's platform but the seller cannot carry them to another venue. Years of five-star trading history built on Amazon belongs to Amazon's database — not to the merchant who earned it.

How can merchants make their reputation portable?

Three approaches: (1) Collect reviews on independent platforms (Trustpilot, Google Business) that exist outside any marketplace ecosystem. (2) Regularly export and archive transaction records as verifiable proof of fulfilled orders. (3) Publish schema.org structured data — Organization, AggregateRating — on the merchant's own domain, independently crawlable by search engines and AI agents.

What is independent merchant verification?

Independent merchant verification is a buyer's or agent's ability to confirm a merchant's identity, business legitimacy, and trading history without using a marketplace platform's interface. It requires published verifiable business identity, a provable trading history outside platform silos, and independently checkable trust signals. This is what allows a foreign buyer — or an AI agent — to trust a merchant they find outside a marketplace context.

Can AI buying agents verify merchant reputation?

Yes — but only if the merchant's reputation is structured and independently accessible. AI buying agents evaluate merchants using machine-readable data: schema.org structured markup, independently crawlable review data, and verifiable identity signals. A merchant whose entire reputation lives inside a platform's proprietary system provides no independently parseable trust data. A merchant with structured data on their own domain is verifiable without platform mediation.
