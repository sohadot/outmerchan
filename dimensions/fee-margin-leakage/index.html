Fee & Margin Leakage

What This Dimension Means

Fee & Margin Leakage measures how much of your gross margin funds the intermediaries on your route to the buyer: commissions, advertising, processing, fulfillment, returns, and the costs that never appear on one invoice. It asks whether you know your full route-to-buyer cost per sale — and whether that route is profitable at all.

Why It Matters to Merchant Sovereignty

A product can be profitable while its route to market is not. Leakage is the quiet form of capture: the merchant keeps operating, keeps selling, keeps growing volume — while the margin that should compound into their independence compounds into the infrastructure's revenue instead. Unmeasured leakage is not a cost; it is a transfer of ownership in slow motion.

The Six Channels of Margin Leakage

01 / Marketplace Commission

Referral fee, selling fee, or category commission. Often the most visible cost, but rarely the largest as a share of margin when all other channels are summed.

02 / Advertising

Paid discovery cost: search ads, sponsored listings, display spend. The most elastic channel — and the one that most reliably escalates as competition increases on a platform.

03 / Payment Processing

Transaction fee, payment gateway cost, currency conversion spread. Small per-transaction, large at volume. Often overlooked because it appears on a separate invoice from the marketplace commission.

04 / Fulfillment & Logistics

Pick, pack, ship, last-mile delivery, and returns handling. The most variable channel — and the one most affected by product size, weight, and return rate.

05 / Returns

Return rate multiplied by the full cost of a return: shipping both ways, restocking, processing, and any value recovery loss. At high return rates, this single channel can eliminate profitability entirely.

06 / Platform-Specific Costs

Storage fees, subscription fees, compliance costs, promotional requirements, and any cost that exists only because of the platform's rules — not the underlying economics of the sale.

How the Score Measures It

The Merchant Sovereignty Score assesses this dimension through 2 governed questions, weighted at 12.5% of the total score. The questions are fixed under public score governance — they change only through a recorded decision, never silently:

  • What share of your gross margin goes to intermediaries — commissions, ads, processing, fulfillment?
  • Do you know your full route-to-buyer cost per sale?

Each answer is scored from captured behavior (0 points) to sovereign behavior (maximum points). The dimension score is the share of sovereign points earned, from 0 to 100.

Low Control vs. High Control

What low control looks like

More than half of gross margin goes to intermediaries. The full route-to-buyer cost has never been calculated. Fee increases are absorbed silently because no alternative route exists to compare against.

What high control looks like

Intermediaries take less than a fifth of gross margin. Route-to-buyer cost is measured per sale and monitored continuously. Every fee on the route is a known, compared, and chosen cost — not an absorbed tax.

The Route-to-Buyer Cost Audit

The route-to-buyer cost audit is the foundational analysis this dimension requires. For one representative sale: add the marketplace commission, the advertising cost allocated to that sale (total ad spend / orders), the payment processing fee, the fulfillment and shipping cost, the return allowance (total returns cost / total orders), and any other per-sale platform cost. Divide the total by the gross margin on that sale — not the revenue. This ratio is the leakage rate.

Most merchants who do this calculation for the first time find a number they cannot unsee. The audit does not change the economics — it makes them visible. Visibility is the prerequisite for sovereignty: you cannot choose a different route if you do not know what the current route costs.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is fee and margin leakage for merchants?

Fee and margin leakage is the cumulative share of a merchant's gross margin that funds intermediaries on the route to the buyer: marketplace commissions, advertising costs, payment processing fees, fulfillment charges, return allowances, and hidden costs across multiple invoices. Leakage is measured against gross margin, not revenue, because revenue-percentage disguises the true impact on profitability.

What is the total cost of selling on a marketplace like Amazon?

The total route-to-buyer cost on a marketplace typically includes: referral fees (6–45% of sale price by category), fulfillment fees, advertising spend to maintain visibility, return processing costs, storage fees, and promotional discounting. When all channels are aggregated, the total commonly ranges from 35% to 65% of gross margin for a fully platform-dependent merchant, depending on category and competition level.

How do I calculate my route-to-buyer cost?

For one representative sale: add the marketplace commission, advertising cost allocated to that sale (total ad spend ÷ orders), payment processing fee, fulfillment and shipping cost, return allowance (total returns cost ÷ orders), and any other per-sale platform cost. Divide the total by the gross margin on that sale — not the revenue. The result is the leakage rate: the share of gross margin that funded intermediaries rather than the merchant.

What percentage of margin should go to intermediaries?

The Merchant Sovereignty Score considers under 20% of gross margin going to intermediaries to be high control, and over 50% to be the captured range. But the most critical variable is not the specific percentage — it is whether the leakage rate is known, measured, and chosen. A merchant who knows their 35% leakage rate is in a materially different position than one who has never calculated it.

What happens when marketplace fees increase?

For a merchant fully dependent on a single marketplace, a fee increase is absorbed silently — no alternative route, no negotiating position. For a merchant with real alternative channels, a fee increase creates a routing decision: accept the new rate or shift volume where margins are better. The difference is not the fee increase itself — it is whether alternatives exist.

How is fee and margin leakage measured in the Merchant Sovereignty Score?

Fee & Margin Leakage is dimension 05 (FEE), weighted at 12.5% of the total. Two governed questions assess it: (1) What share of your gross margin goes to intermediaries? (2) Do you know your full route-to-buyer cost per sale? Each is scored from captured behavior (high unknown leakage) to sovereign behavior (low measured leakage with real routing alternatives).
