Governance Independence

What This Dimension Means

Governance Independence measures who writes the rules of your commerce: you, or a terms-of-service document that changes without your consent. It asks how many parties can rewrite your operating conditions unilaterally — and whether you hold a documented exit path from each of them.

The dimension code is GOV. It is assessed through two governed questions: who can change the conditions of your commerce without asking you, and whether you have a documented exit from each critical dependency. These questions sound simple. Most merchants who answer them honestly discover that several parties hold unilateral authority over dimensions of their commerce that they believed were their own.

Why Governance Is the Deepest Dimension

Every other dimension of the Merchant Sovereignty Score measures a specific operational risk: platform dependency, customer ownership, reputation portability, payment exposure, margin leakage, cross-border trust, machine legibility. Governance Independence is different. It measures the rule structure underneath all of those — who decides the conditions in which the merchant operates each one.

A merchant can diversify channels, own customer data, hold multiple payment rails, and build machine-readable infrastructure — and still operate entirely inside rule systems that others rewrite at will. If the marketplace changes its fee structure, the channel diversification arithmetic changes. If the payment processor updates its acceptable use policy, the multi-rail strategy may lose a rail. If the fulfillment partner changes its service terms, the logistics model changes. Governance is the substrate. It is what determines whether every other dimension is stable or contingent.

Sovereignty's final test is not whether you can operate today, but whether you decide the terms on which you operate tomorrow. This dimension measures that.

The Invisible Governance Trap: Most merchants believe they operate their own commerce. In practice, they operate under a stack of unilateral agreements they accepted during onboarding and have not reviewed since. Each such agreement is a governance transfer — the merchant accepted another party's right to rewrite that domain of their commerce. The trap is invisible because nothing bad is happening right now. The agreements only become visible when the party that holds them exercises the rights they contain.

The Four Categories of Unilateral Control

Governance dependence accumulates through four categories of external control. Most merchants are exposed in more than one.

Marketplace Governance

Fee structures, listing rules, content policies, product category permissions, search ranking criteria, Buy Box eligibility, suspension rights, and payout timing — all defined by the marketplace's terms, changeable without notice or consent.

Payment Processor Governance

Acceptable use policies that determine which products can be sold, reserve requirements that affect cash flow, chargeback policies that determine dispute outcomes, and account termination rights that can freeze funds immediately.

Fulfillment Partner Governance

Geographic service reach, SKU restrictions, storage limits and surcharges, SLA definitions, and pricing models — set by partner contracts that typically favor the partner's ability to change terms on notice periods shorter than the merchant's ability to migrate.

Platform and Infrastructure Governance

E-commerce platform terms (what can be built, what integrations are permitted, what data the merchant can export), advertising platform policies (what can be promoted, how campaigns are structured, when accounts can be suspended), and any SaaS tool whose terms define operational limits.

The Exit Path as Sovereignty Instrument

The exit path is the mechanism that converts dependency into leverage. A merchant who depends on a marketplace but has a documented, tested exit path is in a structurally different position from a merchant who depends on the same marketplace with no exit plan. The first merchant's dependency has a cost ceiling — the cost of executing the exit. The second merchant's dependency has no ceiling. They are subject to any terms the marketplace cares to impose.

An exit path that has been written and documented is leverage. An exit path that has been tested — by actually opening the alternative channel, running a transaction through the backup processor, migrating a portion of inventory — is sovereignty. Testing reveals what the plan assumes incorrectly. An untested exit path is a plan that will fail under the conditions that make it necessary, because those conditions are always the most disruptive.

The OutMerchant standard requires a documented exit path for every critical dependency — not a vague intention to diversify, but a specific sequence: what moves first, what dependencies that step has, what the timeline is, what the revenue impact estimate is during transition. Governance Independence cannot be measured without that specificity.

Governance Independence and AI Commerce

As AI agents become a significant routing layer in commerce, governance independence acquires a new dimension. Platforms building AI recommendation engines are also building new governance layers — they determine which merchants their AI selects, on what criteria, at what cost. A merchant whose visibility depends on a platform's AI is subject to the platform's AI governance: the rules that determine when they appear, when they are deprioritized, and when the model's criteria change.

The sovereign response is the same as in every other dimension: build the infrastructure that makes the merchant selectable without platform intermediation. A merchant who is machine-legible and independently verifiable — whose catalog, terms, and trust signals are openly structured — is governed by open standards, not platform algorithms. Open standards do not hold governance authority over the merchant's commerce in the way a proprietary AI layer does.

How the Score Measures It

The Merchant Sovereignty Score assesses this dimension through 2 governed questions, weighted at 12.5% of the total score. The questions are fixed under public score governance — they change only through a recorded decision, never silently:

  • Who can change the rules of your commerce without your consent?
  • Do you have a documented exit path from each critical provider you depend on?

Each answer is scored from captured behavior (0 points) to sovereign behavior (maximum points). The dimension score is the share of sovereign points earned, from 0 to 100.

Low Control vs. High Control

What low control looks like

Several providers can change fees, policies, or access at any time, with no notice and no negotiation. Dependencies have never been mapped. There is no exit plan from anything. The merchant discovers new governance constraints by being subject to them.

What high control looks like

All critical dependencies are mapped. Every provider whose failure or hostility could stop the business has a documented, tested exit path. The merchant knows exactly what they would do, in what order, in what timeframe. Operating conditions are not a surprise — they are a known position with known alternatives.

One Next Action

List every external party whose unilateral decision could change your operating conditions or stop your business. Write the names. Then pick the single most critical one — the dependency whose adverse action would hurt most — and write a one-page exit sequence this month. An exit you have documented is leverage. That document is the starting point for sovereignty on this dimension.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is governance independence for merchants?
Governance independence is a merchant's condition in which they — not external platforms, payment processors, or intermediaries — set the core operating rules of their commerce. A governance-independent merchant knows exactly who can change their operating conditions, has mapped every such dependency, and holds a documented exit path from each one. The absence of that knowledge and those exit paths is governance dependence.
What is platform terms of service risk for merchants?
Platform terms of service risk is the exposure created when a merchant's operating conditions — fees, permitted product categories, content policies, customer communication rules, payout schedules — are defined by a platform's terms that can change without the merchant's consent. Because marketplace and platform agreements are unilateral contracts, any party whose terms the merchant accepts holds governance authority over that dimension of commerce. When those terms change adversely, the merchant has no contractual recourse — only the option to exit.
What is a merchant exit path?
A merchant exit path is a documented, pre-planned sequence of actions for migrating away from a critical provider — a marketplace, payment processor, fulfillment partner, or platform — in a defined timeframe without catastrophic revenue disruption. An exit path that exists only as an intention is not a sovereign instrument. An exit path that has been written, reviewed, and tested is leverage: it means the merchant does not need the provider on the provider's terms.
Who controls the rules of commerce for online merchants?
For most online merchants, the rules of commerce are controlled by a combination of parties: marketplaces (fee structures, listing rules, content policies, suspension rights), payment processors (acceptable use policies, reserve requirements, chargeback rules), fulfillment partners (service terms, geographic reach, pricing models), and advertising platforms (campaign policies, bidding rules, account eligibility). Each party whose terms the merchant accepts holds unilateral authority over its domain. Governance independence measures the degree to which the merchant has mapped, managed, and prepared exits from each of these control points.
What does governance independence mean in the Merchant Sovereignty Score?
In the Merchant Sovereignty Score, governance independence is dimension 08 (GOV), weighted at 12.5% of the total score. It is assessed through two governed questions: who can change your operating conditions without your consent, and whether you hold a documented exit path from each critical dependency. A score of 0 means the merchant cannot name who controls their operating conditions. A score of 100 means every dependency is mapped and every critical one has a tested exit path.
How do I reduce platform governance risk as a merchant?
To reduce platform governance risk: first, list every external party whose decision could change your operating conditions or stop your business — marketplaces, processors, platforms, fulfillment providers. Second, for each one, write a one-page exit document: what you would do, in what order, in what timeframe, if that provider became hostile or unavailable. Third, test at least the most critical exit path by actually executing part of it — open the alternative account, run a transaction through the backup rail, move a portion of inventory. An exit you have tested is sovereignty; an exit you have documented is leverage; an exit you have only imagined is neither.
